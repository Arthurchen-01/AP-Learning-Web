2015 SCORING GUIDELINES 
Question 1 
General Considerations 
1.
If points are not presented in order, students must clearly indicate to which part of the question
they are responding.
2.
All responses must be written in complete sentences.
3.
A correct definition alone cannot score; students must establish a connection to the prompt
for each point.
4.
A definition may contribute to the answer but the student must also provide an application
of the definition.
5.
Since definitions do not score, if a student provides an incorrect definition but a correct
application the point should score.
6.
Students will not be penalized for misinformation unless it directly contradicts correct
information that would otherwise have scored a point.
7.
Rubric examples provided for each point are not to be considered exhaustive.
8.
Students cannot use a term alone to express the meaning or application of the terms.
9.
Circular definitions will not be accepted; this is considered parroting the question and does
not add any meaning to the question.
Point 1: Cerebellum 
A student’s response must link fine motor coordination, balance, voluntary movement, integrating sensory 
information, implicit memory or procedural memory to playing the guitar. 
Examples: 
•
As Spencer continues to practice, he will be able to play the guitar without having to pay conscious
attention to where he places his fingers. 
•
Damage to the cerebellum disrupts one's fine motor coordination, balance or procedural memory
causing deficits to playing the guitar. 
•
Because Spencer often stands as he plays the guitar, his cerebellum will help him maintain his balance
as he performs.

Question 1 (continued) 
Point 2: Difference Threshold 
A student’s response must explain that Spencer must be able to discriminate between the sounds 
of different chords, musical notes, or instruments OR between different volume levels/decibels. 
Examples: 
•
If Spencer has a low difference threshold he will be better able to distinguish between a B-flat and a B,
making it easier for him to learn how to play. 
•
The more people who are playing guitar in Spencer's band, the more change that will be necessary to
detect a change in the musical notes. Spencer can easily detect those changes, so he will have 
an easier time learning to play. 
•
In order to learn how to play, Spencer needs to play at a higher volume than his instructor does
in order to distinguish the difference between notes. 
DO NOT SCORE absolute thresholds in which a student moves from silence to hearing some sound. 
Point 3: Overjustification Effect 
A student’s response must have ALL of the following: 
A. The task (playing the guitar) must initially be intrinsically motivating; and, 
B. The task must be rewarded or excessively rewarded; and, 
C. The reward or excessive reward must lead the individual to complete the task for the reward, 
rather than for the interest/love of the task alone. 
Note: A response that uses the reduction of Spencer's interest or "desire to perform" will score 
because it alludes to his intrinsic motivation for the task. 
Examples: 
•
Because Spencer is getting money from his father for something that he already loves,
his intrinsic motivation to play the guitar declines. 
•
His instructor no longer rewards him for every little achievement so that Spencer doesn't lose
his love of playing. 
•
If Spencer starts playing "gigs" and is paid well for playing he may begin to see this as a job
and no longer do it for the love of playing music.

Point 4: Explicit Memory 
A student’s response must explain how effortful, conscious recall of an episodic or semantic 
memory relates to playing the guitar, e.g., particular notes, songs, musical content; OR indicate 
that the effortful/conscious processing of acquiring or retrieving allows him to improve his performance 
Examples: 
•
Since Spencer is new to playing the guitar, he has to concentrate to remember the notes
of the song he is playing. 
•
Because Spencer has purposely tried to memorize the notes to the songs he has been playing,
he can tell them to his guitar teacher as he plays them. 
•
Spencer remembers when his instructor yelled at him about how not holding the guitar
correctly will prevent his being able to play properly. 
DO NOT SCORE uses explicit memory to songs he played in the past. 
Point 5: Variable-Ratio Schedule of Reinforcement 
A student’s response must indicate ALL of the following: 
•
Correctly describe being reinforced after an unpredictable number of responses
•
Actions
•
The response either stays the same or increases.
Examples: 
•
If Spencer's father instead only gives him money after an unpredictable number of practices,
then it will help to ensure Spencer continues to practice.
•
Spencer continues practicing because occasionally when he hits the "A" note perfectly,
he feels proud.
DO NOT SCORE any examples that refer specifically to time or a continuous schedule of reinforcement. 
In Part B, students must demonstrate how the concept is related to Spencer's decision to either pursue  
a music career or seek admission to music school.

Question 1 (continued) 
Point 6: Belief Perseverance 
A student’s response must indicate ALL of the following: 
A. The individual holds a belief/conviction; and, 
B. the individual is faced with contradictory evidence; and 
C. the individual maintains the original belief. 
Examples: 
•
Even though Spencer's guitar teacher told him that he is not a very good musician, he continues to
believe that he can succeed in the music industry and continues to pursue a career in music. 
•
Spencer believes that he is not a good musician. He has made straight "A's" in his music classes and
won numerous competitions, but chooses to not apply to music school because he still believes he is 
not any good. 
Point 7: Permissive Parenting Style 
A student’s response must indicate the lack of control or demand by the parent(s), either exemplifying the 
indulgent or rejecting/neglectful style. 
Examples: 
•
Since Spencer's father never gave him guidance regarding decision-making, he won't know how to
make a decision of whether to go to music school. 
•
Spencer's mother gave into his every desire; because of that, he cannot make decisions on his own and
will attend a local college rather than live on his own and pursue a musical career immediately. 
•
Because Spencer's parents have not been actively involved in his life; he has had to make tough
decisions for himself and now decides to move away from home to pursue a career in music. 
DO NOT SCORE responses related to Authoritarian or Authoritative parenting

chooses to get a good paying job instead of pursuing a career in music.

simultaneously)

15-year-olds, simultaneously, rather than using the same group of children at different ages.

Student must refer to measurable behavioral outcome.

It allows for replication of the study.

Student must refer to external validity concerns. 
Examples: 
•
The researcher should randomly select a sample.
•
The study should involve proportionately balanced representations (racial, ethnic, gender,
and socioeconomic status) based on the study's population.
•
The sample should have a sufficient number of participants.
•
The researchers should use a more natural or ecological setting rather than a laboratory setting.

Question 2 (continued) 
Point 6: Critique 
Student’s example must include a criticism of Piagetian theory. 
Examples: 
•
Piaget has been criticized for underestimating the abilities of younger children, so it is possible that
the 5-year-olds had the same ability to decentrate and conserve as the 10-year-olds. 
•
There is much more age variability in acquiring cognitive abilities than Piaget had theorized.
Therefore, 5-year-olds might overlap more with 10-year-olds than Piaget had believed. 
•
Piaget does not include sociocultural factors involved in learning.
Point 7: Reasoning ability 
Student’s example must be based on Piagetian theory. 
Examples: 
•
The 15-year-olds can think abstractly. They can use abstract reasoning to solve a problem.
For example, they might be able to figure out possible solutions to a math problem without trying 
them out with actual objects. 
•
The 15-year-olds can reason inferentially, such as solving the problem, "If Andi arrived later than Sue,
and Heather arrived later than Andi, who arrived the latest?" 
•
The l5-year-olds can use hypothetical-deductive reasoning. If they need to solve a problem, such as
how to drop an egg without breaking it, they can come up with a theory of things that protect the egg 
from breaking and then think then out or try them to solve the problem.

The College Board 
The College Board is a mission-driven not-for-profit organization that connects students to college 
success and opportunity. Founded in 1900, the College Board was created to expand access to 
higher education. Today, the membership association is made up of over 6,000 of the world’s leading 
educational institutions and is dedicated to promoting excellence and equity in education. Each year, 
the College Board helps more than seven million students prepare for a successful transition to college 
through programs and services in college readiness and college success ----- including the SAT® and  
the Advanced Placement Program®. The organization also serves the education community through 
research and advocacy on behalf of students, educators, and schools. The College Board is committed  
to the principles of excellence and equity, and that commitment is embodied in all of its programs, 
services, activities, and concerns.
