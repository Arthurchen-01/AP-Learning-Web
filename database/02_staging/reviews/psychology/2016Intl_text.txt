for the free-response questions in this exam.

Question 1 
General Considerations 
1. Answers must be presented in sentences, and sentences must be cogent enough for the student’s 
meaning to come through. Spelling and grammatical mistakes do not reduce a student’s score, but 
spelling must be close enough that the reader is convinced of the word. 
2. Do not score students' notes made on the question section of the booklet. Score only what has been 
written in the blanks provided in the booklet. 
3. Definitions alone will not score, but they may be used to enhance the application.  
4. Within a point, a student will not be penalized for misinformation unless it directly contradicts correct 
information that would otherwise have scored a point. A correct application with incorrect definition 
is not considered a direct contradiction and should score the point. 
5. Rubric examples provided for each point are not to be considered exhaustive. 
6. A student can score points only if the student clearly conveys what part of the question is being 
answered. It is possible to infer the part of the question being answered if it is consistent with the 
order of the question.  
7. The appropriate context of each section (A and B) must be explicitly established or related back to 
the prompt of the question. 
Part A 
Point 1: Explain how the design of this study is experimental.   
Participants are randomly assigned to one of two different conditions OR there is a manipulation of 
the independent variable. A complete description of the manipulation procedure would score, even if 
the word “manipulation” is not used.  
• 
• 
• 
• 
• 
Example: Participants were randomly assigned to the “Lucky Ball Group” or the other group. 
Example: The experimental group, the one receiving the treatment, was told they had the lucky 
ball.   
Example: The control group was not told that their ball had any lucky properties. 
Random selection and random sampling DO NOT SCORE. 
Simply stating that there was a control group DOES NOT SCORE. 
Point 2: Explain what it means to say that there is a statistically significant difference between these two 
groups.  
The difference between the groups is less likely/has a low probability of being due to chance/random 
variation/coincidence/Type I error OR the difference between the groups is more likely/ has a high 
probability of being due to the manipulation of the independent variable.  
• 
• 
• 
Example: The researchers were 95% sure that the IV caused the difference between the 
performance of the groups. 
Example: The difference was not due to chance. 
Responses that use p < .05 alone DO NOT SCORE.

Point 3: Explain the possible relationship between superstitious beliefs and self-efficacy.  
Superstitious beliefs boost one’s belief that they can master a task OR one’s belief that they will have an 
increase in their personal skill level or capability. Increased confidence in ability should clearly reside 
within the person and not be ascribed to the ball. 
• 
• 
• 
Example: Believing that one had the lucky ball, led to greater certainty that they would get more 
balls into the hole. 
Self-esteem definitions and “Self-fulfilling prophecy” alone DO NOT SCORE. 
References only to improved performance DO NOT SCORE. 
Part B: Explain how each of the following may lead to the development or maintenance of superstitious 
beliefs or behaviors. 
Note: Each response must reference the concept of superstition (any logically unrelated pairing) but does not 
need to refer to golf or lucky socks. 
Point 4: Illusory Correlation (perception of a relationship that does not exist) 
Must mention that a relationship is perceived AND that the relationship is noncontingent, 
coincidental, false, or occurring by chance. 
• 
• 
Example: Jordan wears purple socks to a game and has a great game. Jordan incorrectly assumes 
that the socks must have led to success, so Jordan wears them to every game. 
Example: This concept leads to the development of superstitions because it appears that the 
superstitious behavior is causing the increased performance, when in reality it is another factor. 
Point 5: Positive Reinforcement (Any positive outcome that strengthens the likelihood of a behavior 
occurring in the future) 
Responses that score must reference:  
• 
• 
• 
Performing a random/superstitious behavior  
Desirable stimulus/outcome following the random/superstitious behavior  
Increases the likelihood of the superstitious behavior occurring in the future.  
• 
• 
Example: I happened to wear my purple socks and my team won. Now I wear my purple socks for 
EVERY GAME! 
Strengthen the superstitious belief DOES NOT SCORE. Must indicate the behavior is 
strengthened.

Point 6: External Locus of Control (belief that circumstances out of one’s control are responsible for 
one’s success) 
External circumstances (could include a powerful entity, chance, or luck) are believed to influence a 
logically unrelated result. Responses that score could refer to locus of control as either a state or as a trait. 
• 
• 
• 
Example: If you have an external locus of control, you are more susceptible to having superstitious 
beliefs, like wearing lucky socks, because the socks cause you to get a good grade on an exam.  
Example: The Boston Red Sox had an external locus of control if they believed a curse kept them 
from winning a World Series for almost 100 years. 
Stating “external” alone without identifying a source of control DOES NOT SCORE. 
Point 7: Episodic Memory (recall of specific personal events or experiences) 
Responses that score must reference an individual’s memory, recall, or remembering AND include 
reference to superstitious beliefs or behaviors 
• 
Example: You recall only those baseball games in which you hit home runs whenever you wore 
your purple socks. As a result, you wear your purple socks for every game because you remember 
that success.

the admissions committee therefore decides to keep the exam.”
Note: The direction of the predictive validity must correctly impact the decision to eliminate or
retain the admissions exam. (High predictive validity contributes to exam retention; low predictive
validity contributes to exam discontinuation.)

Increased female enrollment or interest
